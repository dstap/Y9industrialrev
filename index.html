Historical Context

Social

Prior to the industrial revolution, the people of Great Britain itself were experiencing the agricultural revolution. All unwealthy citizens of Great Britain didn’t live in urbanised homes but rather rural cottages, they often worked as hired labour or sought employment in town. Labourers were adults and children alike. Additionally, jobs were mostly seasonal and peasants who sought education for their child were unable to receive it unlike children who were a part of a wealthy family and could be privately tutored. Girls in general were excluded from education until after the age of 7 - very specific. Most peasants lived this social lifestyle as well as merchants and their earnings fluctuated negatively at some points “The advent of industrial development revamped patterns of human settlement, labour, and family life. The changes set in motion by industrialization ushered Europe, the United States of America, and much of the world into the modern era..”
(Nat Geo) Industrialisation, Labour and Life


Political

During the industrial revolution politics did not change much, with Britain still relying on a similar hierarchy system today however during this time Britain was an empire which lasted until 1997. Before the industrial revolution there were no taxes with taxes only being introduced to Britain in 1799. With King George III and Queen Victoria ruling before and during the industrial revolution, as well as having 22 different Prime Ministers during this time. Additionally there were no existing civil rights to protect labourers of any type “The role of Parliament in these complex developments was often crucial, and a great deal of regulatory legislation was put in place.”
Parliament.Uk


Cultural

Before the industrial revolution, the overall way of life was differential and similar in certain aspects. People still lived amongst each other but rarely left their medium-sized villages in the “British isles” and the simple individuals led “agrarian lifestyles”. Culturally, peasants also practised subsistence farming as their ancestors did to meet basic living standards, this was practised amongst the community.(Nat Geo) Industrialisation, Labour and Life


Economic

Prior to the industrial revolution, Britain relied a lot on farming and artisan crafts(a trade where one often uses their hands to make things). There was not a lot of trading before the revolution and because of this people often relied on others in their community for food and other supplies. Communities would have to work together and share out the jobs depending on what they need with common jobs like farmers, bakers and butches for food and other jobs like blacksmiths, carpenter, leather workers and weavers(also know as textile workers).” Before industrialization, when the most significant economic activities in most European countries were small-scale farming and artisan handicrafts, social structures remained essentially as they had been during the Middle Ages.”
(Nat Geo) Industrialisation, Labour and Life

Process and Decline

Process

Newcomen’s Steam engine brought about efficiency in mines, this steam power was first applied to pump water out of coal mines to thoroughly empty coal seams of water to eliminate danger and dangerous labour to miners. Though this machine was incredibly inefficient and limited in terms of capabilities. Later however, this was utilised in factories, workplaces and were integrated into Steam-locomotives as well as factory grade machinery. This brought about efficiency and commenced England’s industrial revolution! It was then optimised by James Watt and Matthew Boulton, Watt’s engine achieved high levels of efficiency and mechanised European Industry in all aspects. This brought drastic economic growth to england and enabled larger enterprises to establish themselves “The steam engine turned the wheels of mechanised factory production. Its emergence freed manufacturers from the need to locate their factories on or near sources of water power. Large enterprises began to concentrate in rapidly growing industrial cities.”
Industrial Revolution and Technology | National Geographic Society


Decline

“Water power was a more popular energy source for grinding grain and other types of mill work in most of preindustrial Europe. This integral aspect of the industrial revolution also prompted the extreme demand for coal which also degraded the environment and created unsafe work environments, forced labourers to work in factories such as the factories and mines; which had the risk of explosions despite whilst also prompting the production of goods and the contribution to the domestic system. So considering the Steam Engine’s permanent damage to society, most profoundly to the workplaces (mines/factories)in this way. What are the benefits of fair trade to the producer and the consumer?

Fair trade is the most trusted sustainability-label in the world, they ensures that through the production/manufacturing of a product, that it’s traded under genuine and legitimate circumstances and conditions. It changes the way it works through suitable prices, decent working and liveable conditions for workers in developing countries. More importantly, this global leader in this related movement ensures that individuals involved in such businesses are able to act and make decisions that influence the future of their business and product. This applies to textile-makers in this specific case and even agricultural workers (employers or employees).In general, a fairtrade product means that the decisions resulting in its production and selling have been enabled by positive, self and collective-sustaining decisions.“A product with the FAIRTRADE Mark means producers and businesses have met internationally agreed standards which have been independently certified. Farmers and workers have a strong voice at every level of Fairtrade, from how they invest in and run their local organisations to having an equal say in Fairtrade’s global decision-making.”
Fairtrade.net - What is Fair-trade?


What are the benefits of fair trade to the producer and the consumer?

The benefits of fair trade to the producer means that more income is able to be made to ensure the high quality and quantity of a good which can be distributed thanks to the stable income created, this benefits the consumer through providing them with certified genuine products of sustainable origin. Also, In terms of the producer, they’re being benefited through being able to work in suitable conditions as legal labourers. Overall, Fairtrade’s ultimate Goal, which is to eliminate poverty and mutually benefits both the producer and consumer as it guarantees the output of quality and decently-priced products to corroborate with consumer satisfactory standards whilst simultaneously maintaining workable environments for employers and employees in all circumstances.
